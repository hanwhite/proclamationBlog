Marital Sexuality and Fertility
Sex and intimacy between husband and wife represent the physical and spiritual oneness between them. Sex within the bonds of marriage should be treated as a sacred, intimate connection authorized by God to fulfill His commandments and progress together as husband and wife. In Successful Marriages and Families: Proclamation Principles and Research Perspectives, sex within the bonds of marriage is described as serving “several purposes for both husband and wife as individuals as well as for the couple relationship. These purposes include becoming one, connecting with God, strengthening the emotional and spiritual bonds in marriage, avoiding temptation, and continuing the generational chain by bringing children into a family.”
On the subject of becoming one with your spouse, David A. Bednar said, “The natures of male and female spirits complete and perfect each other, and therefore men and women are intended to progress together toward exaltation.” Intimacy is an essential part of this progression. It is worth noting that Elder Bednar specifies “male and female spirits”. Our gender is not only determined by our biological, physical bodies, but by our spirits as well. Male and female spirits are meant to complete and connect with each other, spiritually as well as physically. As the adversary continually attempts to make sex more casual in the world, it is imperative that we continue to teach about and treat it with sacredness and reverence. The purpose of connecting with God through martial sex and intimacy is described by Elder Jeffery R. Holland, who stated, “Sexual intimacy is … symbolic of a union between mortals and deity, between otherwise ordinary and fallible humans uniting for a rare and special moment with God Himself and all the powers by which He gives life in this wide universe of ours.”
One purpose of sex that is agreed upon by most of the world is that it is used to strengthen and express connection and love for one’s spouse. It is important to understand that the act of sex is not in and of itself a sin, but it can be used in a sinful way. Sex is more than a way to find individual physical pleasure. Sexual intimacy should only be used between husband and wife as a way to respectfully, lovingly, and intimately connect with each other and God.
One of God’s first commandments was to multiply and replenish the Earth (Genesis 1:28). There are many ways to achieve and follow this commandment. The first is sexual intimacy in a marriage to bring life into the world with biological children. However, there are many situations involving infertility that can prevent a couple from bringing children into their family this way. This can impact the number of children they desire to have and the timing involved. Temple sealings allow married couples to fulfill this commandment in other ways by bringing children into their family through adoption. In Successful Marriages and Families: Proclamation Principles and Research Perspectives, it also explains that those who are physically unable to have children are still entitled to blessings through adoption, and “the Abrahamic blessing of eternal increase is a promise that extends beyond the boundaries of the earth life.” (Harper and Feinaur, Chapter 5). The decision of when and how to have children is a sacred decision that is made between a husband, wife, and God only. Outside opinions do not apply to a couple’s decision on when and how they want to have children. Every situation, and every couple, are unique. Elder Dallin H. Oaks stated, “How many children should a couple have? All they can care for! Of course, to care for children means more than simply giving them life. Children must be loved, nurtured, taught, fed, clothed, housed, and well started in their capacities to be good parents themselves.” It is also important to consider the physical and mental health of both the husband and the wife when deciding the timing and amount of children they can have. There are no black-and-white guidelines to follow when it comes to this decision. It is a situation to use agency, prayer, and thoughtful discussions with one’s spouse in order to come to a conclusion based on a couple’s circumstances.
With the establishment that sexual intercourse in a marriage is extremely important and necessary comes the question of how often a husband and wife should perform this sacred act. According to David Schnarch, a licensed clinical psychologist Board Certified in Couple and Family Psychology, “there is always a low desire partner, just as there is always a high desire partner - and there is one of each in every relationship.” He states that though husband and wife may want the same thing, one will want it more often than the other. The difference in the level of desire may vary from couple to couple, and some may not recognize it as a pertinent issue. However for those that do, it can become a stressful and anxious topic, resulting in those feelings being tied to intimacy. This is where the “good enough sex” approach can be introduced. “Good enough sex” is a term coined by McCarthy and Metz, who teach that martial sexuality should be flexible and pressure associated with sexual intercourse should be taken away from it. This approach teaches that the expectation that sexual interaction should always end in “100 percent satisfactory results” (Harper and Feinaur, Chapter 5) is unrealistic and can feel demanding for one or both spouses. Once spouses can work on “abandoning a goal of ‘perfect performance’” (Harper and Feinaur, Chapter 5) they are able to work on getting over the fears, stress, and pressures that can be associated with intercourse, performances, and actions by feeling relaxed about the subject. Marital sexuality requires that spouses learn to be open and honest with each other about their feelings and experiences, and with that comes trust within the couple.
